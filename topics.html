Solar System Formation

Learn how our solar system formed 4.6 billion years ago

The solar system formed from a giant cloud of gas and dust called a solar nebula. About 4.6 billion years ago, gravity caused this cloud to collapse and form a spinning disk. The center became the Sun, while the outer material formed planets, moons, asteroids, and comets.

Key processes included accretion (particles sticking together), differentiation (planets separating into layers), and planetary migration. Terrestrial planets formed closer to the Sun where only rock and metal could condense, while gas giants formed further out where ice could also exist.

Life Cycle of Stars

Understand how stars are born, live, and die

Stars form when dense regions in molecular clouds collapse under gravity. Nuclear fusion begins when the core reaches 10 million K, starting the main sequence phase. Most of a star's life (billions of years) is spent on the main sequence, fusing hydrogen into helium.

After exhausting hydrogen, low-mass stars become red giants, then white dwarfs. High-mass stars become supergiants and explode as supernovae, leaving behind neutron stars or black holes. These explosions create heavier elements and seed the universe with materials for new stars and planets.

Black Holes and Relativity

Explore the mysteries of black holes and Einstein's theories

Black holes are regions where gravity is so strong that nothing, not even light, can escape. They form when massive stars collapse at the end of their lives. Einstein's theory of general relativity predicts their existence and describes how they warp spacetime.

Supermassive black holes, millions to billions of times the Sun's mass, exist at the centers of most galaxies. The event horizon marks the point of no return, while the singularity at the center has infinite density. Black holes can merge, producing gravitational waves detectable on Earth.

Exoplanets and Habitability

Discover planets beyond our solar system

Exoplanets are planets orbiting stars other than the Sun. Over 5,000 have been confirmed. Detection methods include the transit method (measuring dimming as planets pass in front of stars) and radial velocity (detecting stellar wobble).

The habitable zone is the region around a star where liquid water could exist on a planet's surface. Factors affecting habitability include star type, planetary atmosphere, magnetic fields, and plate tectonics. Some exoplanets may have conditions suitable for life as we know it.

Earth-Moon System

Learn about Earth's relationship with the Moon

The Moon likely formed from debris ejected when a Mars-sized object collided with early Earth. It stabilizes Earth's axial tilt, preventing extreme climate variations. The Moon's gravity creates tides, which have influenced evolution of life.

Lunar phases occur as the Moon orbits Earth, with different portions illuminated. Eclipses happen when Sun, Earth, and Moon align. The Moon is slowly moving away from Earth at 3.8 cm per year, which will affect Earth's rotation over billions of years.

Space Exploration and Technology

Explore space missions and technology

Space exploration began with Sputnik in 1957 and accelerated with the Apollo Moon landings. Modern missions include Mars rovers, the James Webb Space Telescope, and the International Space Station. These missions advance our understanding of the universe.

Key technologies include rocket propulsion, life support systems, robotics, and remote sensing. Future missions aim for Mars colonization, asteroid mining, and interstellar travel. Space technology has also led to innovations in medicine, communications, and Earth observation.
