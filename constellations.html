CONSTELLATIONS OF THE NIGHT SKY

Learn to identify patterns in the stars. Discover the myths and legends behind these celestial formations that have guided travelers for thousands of years.

ORION

The Hunter. One of the most recognizable constellations. Visible from November to February in the Northern Hemisphere.

Best Visible: Winter (Northern Hemisphere)

Brightest Star: Rigel

Mythology: Greek hunter in the stars

Features: Orion's Belt (3 stars in a row)

URSA MAJOR

The Great Bear. Contains the Big Dipper asterism. Used for navigation throughout history.

Best Visible: Year-round (Northern Hemisphere)

Brightest Star: Alioth

Mythology: Callisto transformed into a bear

Features: The Big Dipper (7 stars)

CASSIOPEIA

The Queen. Forms a distinctive "W" or "M" shape. Visible year-round in Northern latitudes.

Best Visible: Year-round (Northern Hemisphere)

Brightest Star: Schedar

Mythology: Vain queen of Ethiopia

Features: Distinctive W/M shape

LEO

The Lion. One of the zodiac constellations. Contains the bright star Regulus, meaning "little king."

Best Visible: Spring (Northern Hemisphere)

Brightest Star: Regulus

Mythology: Nemean Lion defeated by Hercules

Features: Zodiac constellation

SCORPIUS

The Scorpion. A zodiac constellation with a distinctive curved tail. Contains the bright red star Antares.

Best Visible: Summer (Northern Hemisphere)

Brightest Star: Antares (Red Supergiant)

Mythology: Scorpion that killed Orion

Features: Distinctive scorpion shape

CYGNUS

The Swan. Also known as the Northern Cross. Part of the Summer Triangle along with Vega and Altair.

Best Visible: Summer (Northern Hemisphere)

Brightest Star: Deneb

Mythology: Zeus in swan form

Features: Northern Cross asterism

DRACO

The Dragon. Winds around the Little Dipper. Used to point to the north celestial pole thousands of years ago.

Best Visible: Year-round (Northern Hemisphere)

Brightest Star: Eltanin

Mythology: Dragon slain by Hercules

Features: Wraps around Ursa Minor

PEGASUS

The Winged Horse. Contains the Great Square of Pegasus asterism. Visible in autumn skies.

Best Visible: Autumn (Northern Hemisphere)

Brightest Star: Enif

Mythology: Winged horse from Greek myths

Features: Great Square asterism

ANDROMEDA

The Chained Princess. Contains the Andromeda Galaxy, visible to the naked eye on dark nights.

Best Visible: Autumn (Northern Hemisphere)

Brightest Star: Alpheratz

Mythology: Princess chained to a rock

Features: Andromeda Galaxy (M31)

HOW TO FIND CONSTELLATIONS

TIPS FOR STARGAZING

  • Find a dark location away from city lights
  • Allow 20 minutes for your eyes to adjust to darkness
  • Use star maps or astronomy apps on your phone
  • Start with bright, easily recognizable patterns
  • Use the Big Dipper as a guide to find other stars

SEASONAL VIEWING

Different constellations are visible at different times of year as Earth orbits the Sun. Some constellations circle the North Star and are visible year-round, while others are seasonal.

Winter: Orion, Taurus, Gemini

Spring: Leo, Virgo, Bootes

Summer: Cygnus, Lyra, Scorpius

Autumn: Pegasus, Andromeda, Perseus
